Statement of Purpose

In the digital age, individuals should feel empowered to leverage technology tools to fully participate in society and connect with critical resources, such as healthcare benefits, employment opportunities, and educational resources. Grassroots solutions are crucial to expand digital inclusion and connect communities with technological infrastructure, resources, and skills. With artificial intelligence (AI) transforming the way that companies, online spaces, and government operate, it is essential for community members to gain AI fluency. AI fluency is no longer optional, but a core part of digital inclusion. Without integrating AI fluency into digital inclusion curriculum, AI will exacerbate the digital divide, resulting in the "AI divide."

Why demystifyAI Chicago?

Equipping individuals with AI readiness skills empowers them to engage more confidently in AI-related conversations, recognize and challenge abuses of AI in both personal and civic contexts, and explore how AI can be meaningfully integrated into their personal and professional lives. This task is nonpartisan: technology should not happen TO individuals but rather FOR individuals. Digital literacy efforts already exist in Chicago, but many organizations do not have the resources to integrate AI curriculum into their programming. DemystifyAI fills this gap by providing community members with a practical AI readiness curriculum, which is especially important for nonprofits. Our curriculum is designed with the everyday community member in mind, foregrounding lived experiences and community input to co-create interesting, relevant, and impactful curriculum.

Our Mission

Our mission is to equip Chicago community members with practical AI and digital literacy skills to participate confidently in our AI-integrated society while protecting themselves from AI-related risks. Our target audience – with emphasis on accessibility across income levels, ethnicities, languages, and digital experience levels – includes digital inclusion nonprofit organizations (and subsequently, the communities they serve), and community members whom we are able to reach ourselves. In order for demystifyAI Chicago to serve as many community members as possible, we will pursue two tracks:

  • Track 1 (Nonprofit Co-Creation): Geared toward community members that different digital inclusion nonprofits already serve, allowing us to reach communities for whom we are not immediately accessible (e.g. due to language barriers).
  • Track 2 (Direct Delivery): Direct content delivery to different community members.

The ultimate goal is to develop an open-source repository of resources that can be adapted to fit each community's needs.

Syllabus

demystifyAI Chicago leveraged existing online AI readiness resources to design a base AI literacy curriculum. We engage with community members primarily through Track 1, the nonprofit co-creation and "Train the Trainer" model, which involves tailoring the base curriculum to the partner organization's needs and co-designing with interested nonprofits/community organizations. We also provide training sessions for nonprofit staff, per interest, with the ultimate goal of our base curriculum being something the partner organization can deliver independently. Each organization receives a stipend for working with us, to be used for training and outreach to community members. Track 2, the direct delivery mode, involves demystifyAI Chicago's team directly delivering the syllabus - in form of workshops - to interested community members.

Curriculum can be delivered in three parts or combined and modified given logistical constraints. We currently support the following modules, in addition to pre-session and post-session surveys.

Module 1: AI Foundations

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand what AI actually is (and isn't) in everyday terms
  • Recognize AI in daily life experiences and use cases
  • Distinguish between AI myths and realities

Topics

  • What AI Really Means: Simple definition: AI as pattern recognition and prediction. Common examples: autocorrect, GPS navigation, social media feeds. The basic building blocks of how it works (e.g. trained on data, predictive vs. generative)
  • AI in Your Daily Life: Interactive exercise: Identifying AI in participants' routines. Smartphones, streaming services, online shopping recommendations. Banking fraud detection, email spam filtering
  • Debunking Myths: Importance of human involvement. AI is not magic or all-knowing. AI is not "objective". Discussion: what do participants already know about AI?

Module 2: Protecting Yourself and Engaging with AI

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify common AI-powered scams and fraud attempts
  • Understand deepfakes and manipulated media

Topics

  • Media Literacy, Algorithmic Recommendations, and AI-Enabled Scams and Manipulation: Voice and video deepfakes and media manipulation. AI-generated phishing and scams (e.g. email scams). Algorithmic recommendations and filter bubbles. Discussion: Authenticity verification checklist
  • Privacy and Data Misuse: Manage privacy settings. Data ownership rights and navigating digital consent (cookies, terms of service, etc.). Discussion: Knowing your rights
  • Understanding Applications of Algorithms in the Social and Public Sectors: In employment, healthcare, law enforcement, etc. Algorithmic usage in government settings & how they affect you everyday. Appealing automated decisions. Discussion: ask critical thinking questions about fairness, transparency, and accountability

Module 3: AI as a Tool - Practical Applications for Community Benefit

Learning Outcomes

  • Explore positive uses of AI tools for personal and community needs
  • Hands-on experience with accessible AI applications
  • Understand limitations and responsible use of AI

Topics Covered

  • Prompt Engineering: What is a prompt? Strategies for writing effective prompts. Hallucinations and evaluating LLM responses.
  • Language and Communication Tools: Translation apps for multilingual communities. Text-to-speech and speech-to-text tools. Document analysis (e.g. uploading a pdf and automatic translation)
  • Information and Learning Tools: Use in brainstorming. AI writing assistants. AI tutoring. Accessibility features (image description, closed captioning). Agentic AI and use in daily workflows and searches
  • Hands-On Workshop: Experiment with tools. Discussion of benefits and limitations. How could these tools help your family or community organization? What concerns do you have about using AI tools?

Partner organizations are also provided information to understand how AI is used in government or business decision-making (benefit determination, administrative task efficiency, credit scoring and loan decisions, etc.), relevant laws that govern data and AI (for example, a recent Illinois law was passed banning AI's discriminatory use in employment decisions), and practical ways to advocate for certain policies, file complaints, and share promising use cases of AI to support community initiatives (including free tools and resources).
